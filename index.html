Electronic governor for a petrol engine

For my A-Level Design and Technology moudle in lower sixth, I took on the ambitous project of designing an electronic governor for a petrol engine. The need came about because a lawnmower engine had been squeezed in to the body a scale model locomotive and the mechanical governor had to be romoved to make it fit. The loco was dangerous to drive as it was too highly geared anyway and the engine would very easily race off because the carbourettor wasn't governed. THe first step was to measure the speed of the engine so I made a disc with 8 holes in and attached it to the flywheel. Using a light gate, a comparator, a 555 timer and an averager, I could convert this speed of the engine in to a signal between 0-5V. This was fed in to the Arduino along with the potentiometer reading that the driver uses to set the speed, and with the use fo a servo, adjusts the carbourettor accordingly. I ran in to some issues because when the engine was running, interference from the spark plug made the servo jitter about uncontrolably. Once I've finished university, this is a project that I'd like to pick up again because I am now more experienced so I should be able to shield everything properly and can design a nice control loop

Resonant Peak Tracker

My most recent electrical project at 42 Technology was to design a circuit board that tracks the resonant frequency of a piezo ring. If the piezo ring is oscillating at the correct frequency (about 3.4MHz), then algae suspended in water will get trapped which can then be collected. However, the resonant peak drifts by over 5KHz for every one degree Celsius. I therefore designed a signal generator that will first characterise the piezo by going through the frequency spectrum and measuring the impedance. It will then choose the correct high impedance peak and track it as external variables make the peak drift. I had to be careful when designing the PCB, keeping signal traces as short as possible because parastic capacitances and inductances can caue large impedance changes at these high frequencies.

IOT Torque wrench

In my embedded systems group, we had to make an IOT device using a sensor chosen at random. We received a load cell, and I suggested that we make an IOT torque wrench. In my group, I focussed on the hardware. I bought a breaker bar, sawed it in half and welded a bracket to each half so the load cell could be mounted between them. I then made the breakout board for the pi zero on some stripboard and wired up the LED, the haptic feedback transducer, the buzzer and the load cell. Once all this was complete, I assisted with the main python program that would run on the pi. On power up, the LED would come on prompting a zero reading. The user then holds the wrench with the driving bar dangling down and presses the button. Once this had been complete, the user can select his car make and model from his phone and a database will look up the torque required for the when nuts. The wrench will then light up, buzz and vibrate at the same time as soon as the desired torque has been reached.

Instrumentation

As a 3rd year project, I designed an impedance measuring board that could measure the impedance of capacitors or inductors up to 100kHz. Unlike most of the other boards, no external power supply was needed other than the usb cable to the microcontroller. The device would measure the voltage across the test device using an instrumentation amplifier and then pick the closest resistor to match the impedance from logarithmically increasing resistor values. The voltage and current were measured with the help of some instrumentation amplifiers which was then fed in to the Arduino to calculate the magnitude of impedance. To give the phase, a zero crossing detection circuit transformed the voltage and current waveform in to square waves and the microcontroller timed the duration between the rising edges of each signal.

Power Supply

For a 3rd year project, our pair designed a PCB for a high current (5A) 3.3V power supply. Because of the high current and low voltage, a diode could not be used in the normal way in a buck power supply. This meant that the board had to use synchronous rectification. A suitable MOSFET driver was chosen and then we set about searching for MOSFETs with the lowest “On-Resistance” but with a low gate capacitance. The low gate capacitance and the lowest switching frequency we were allowed (150kHZ) meant that we should have low switching losses. The downside of a low switching frequency is that the ripple current (and therefore output ripple voltage) will be high, so we wound our own inductor to give a high inductance (220uH) which limits the current ripple.
